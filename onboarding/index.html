The First Week

The first week is the most important week of our engagement. It's also protected by a simple guarantee:

If you're not happy after the first week, for any reason, cancel and I'll refund 75%. No questions asked.

That guarantee exists because I'm confident the first week will prove the value of working together. Here's why.

It's a Two-Way Onboarding

The first week is me onboarding onto your product and your team, and you onboarding onto how Crucial.Design works. Both sides need to click.

On my side, I'll spend time going through your existing designs, documentation, product, and anything else that helps me understand what you've built so far. I want to understand the team structure, who makes decisions, how your developers work, and where the real problems are. Not the surface-level stuff. The actual friction.

On your side, you'll get a feel for how I communicate, how I think through problems, and whether this working relationship makes sense long-term.

What the First Week Usually Looks Like

I build for the long term. That means getting the foundations right before moving fast.

For most products, the first week is research and conversations. I'll dig into your product positioning, market context, and the core problem you're solving. Think post-its and discussions, not polished screens. This is the work that makes everything after it faster and sharper.

Designing the wrong thing quickly is worse than understanding the right thing first.

When We Skip Straight to Design

If the problem is already well-defined and there's genuine urgency, we can prioritise design artifacts in week one. Maybe you're on the edge of funding conversations and need an investor deck polished. Maybe there's a specific screen or flow that's blocking a release. If it aligns with your product's success, we'll make it happen.

We'll discuss on the discovery call what your first week will likely look like, so expectations are set before we start.

05

What "An Update" Means

An update is a meaningful unit of design progress. Not hours. Not a fixed number of screens. It's the next thing that moves your product forward.

Examples of one update:

  • A new screen or flow designed end-to-end
  • A set of revisions on previous work based on your feedback
  • A component system or pattern library section
  • A UX audit with annotated recommendations
  • Multiple smaller tweaks shipped together

Some updates are bigger, some smaller. It balances out over the engagement. If something feels off, we talk about it.

Calls vs. Slack

Priority plan clients get a calendar link to book calls with me directly. Here's how to get the most out of it.

Use calls for: Strategic discussions. Complex product decisions. Feedback that's hard to express in text. Alignment on direction. Anything where a live conversation will move things forward faster than typing.

Use Slack for: Status updates. Quick questions. Sharing references or briefs. Feedback on delivered work. Anything where the answer fits in a message.

Simple rule: if it fits in a Slack message, it should be a Slack message.

One call per week is usually the right cadence. Some weeks you won't need one at all. If you find yourself booking multiple calls in a single week, that's usually a sign we should improve our async process instead.

08

Giving Feedback

Clear feedback keeps things moving. Here's what works best:

Do: Describe the problem, not the solution. "This feels cluttered" is more useful than "make the button blue."

Do: Be specific. "The onboarding flow loses people at step 3" beats "this doesn't feel right."

Do: Share context. Loom videos, annotated screenshots, or a quick Slack message all work.

Don't worry about: Having the solution figured out. That's my job.

Revisions vs. New Requests

Revisions are changes to work I've already delivered. Adjusting layouts, refining interactions, reworking a screen based on feedback. That's all part of the process.

A new request is something net-new that wasn't part of the original brief. New requests go into the queue and get picked up in the next update cycle.

If the line feels blurry, just ask. I'd rather we talk about it than have either of us guess.

What I Need From You

This is a collaboration. The quality of the output depends on both of us. Here's what helps me do my best work:

A single point of contact. One person who can make decisions, give feedback, and approve direction. Design by committee kills momentum.

Timely feedback. I aim for feedback within 3 business days. If I'm waiting longer than that, the next update cycle starts anyway. I'd rather keep moving and adjust than sit idle.

Access from day one. Product access, existing design files, brand assets, documentation, codebase if relevant. The faster I have context, the faster I can deliver value. Ideally within 24 hours of kicking off.

Honesty. If something isn't working, tell me. If priorities shift, tell me. If the budget situation changes, tell me. I'd rather hear it early and adapt than find out late.
